You are building The Stomping Path — Zone 2 of the Headwaters watershed system. The Worn Path. Transition.

This is the active zone. The motion zone. If the Shallows (Zone 3) is still water, the Stomping Path is the current that leads there. You are building the trail, not the destination.

What the Stomping Path is
A stomping path is the worn groove of repeated movement. In permaculture: a desire path — the line grass wears down where people actually walk, not where the planner put the sidewalk. In the early internet: your bookmarks list, your daily forum check, the eight sites in the same order every morning, the RSS reader you scanned before coffee.

The Stomping Path is Zone 2 — transition, bench, trail. It sits between the Lodge (Zone 1, daily tools, The Spring) and The Clearing (Zone 3, the gathering place). Water runs through here. People pass through here. The path is how you build relationship with places over time.

The Stomping Path is documentation of repeated movement. It records where people actually go, not where they were told to go.

The emotional target
The worn groove. A stomping path has weight to it — you can feel it underfoot. It has history. It was made by someone walking the same way many times, for their own reasons, until the ground remembered. The Stomping Path should feel like this: something worn smooth by use, not designed smooth by a committee.

Transition, not destination. This is not where you stay. This is how you move. The bench on the trail — you rest here, you orient, you continue. The Stomping Path serves the journey without trying to be the end of it.

The ritual of return. The early internet's best quality was the daily ritual: you had your route, you walked it, the places you visited were waiting where you left them. The Stomping Path honors this. It is built for people who come back, not people who stumble in once.

The desire path over the designed path. The official routes are for strangers. The stomping path is for people who live here. It goes where people actually need to go — through the back, past the fence, the way that's shorter even if it's not on any map.

The shift this zone navigates
The Stomping Path is the transition between two kinds of space:

Zone 1 (The Lodge / The Spring) — daily tools, the operational center, where the work gets done. Functional, practical, interior.
Zone 3 (The Clearing / The Shallows) — gathering, rest, the still place, the club you find by looking. Quiet, ambient, exterior.
The Stomping Path is the membrane between them. It carries the quality of both without being either. It is functional enough to navigate, open enough to breathe. You leave the Lodge with something in your hands. You arrive at the Clearing with your pace changed.

Design for the pace change. The Stomping Path should slow you down just enough that you arrive at the Shallows at the right speed — not still running from the Lodge.

What the path documents
The Stomping Path holds the record of movement through the watershed. This could manifest as:

The routes people actually take — not the prescribed ones, the real ones
The places people return to — frequency, rhythm, the groove
The transitions between states — what changes between Zone 1 and Zone 3, and when
The worn spots — where the most traffic goes, what that reveals about what people actually need
This is documentation in the sense of a naturalist's field notes — observational, specific, without judgment. Not analytics. Not a dashboard. A record of lived movement.

What it should feel like
A trail, not a road. Trails follow the terrain. They bend around obstacles. They have texture underfoot. Roads are imposed. Trails are discovered.
Wayfinding without signage. You know where you are because of the landscape, not because someone put up a sign. Landmarks, not labels.
The bench midway. There should be a resting point — a place to pause, orient, look back at where you came from and forward at where you're going. Not a CTA. A bench.
Seasonal quality. Paths look different in different conditions. The Stomping Path can carry time — a sense that it has been walked before, will be walked again, looks different depending on when you arrive.
Worn texture over polished surface. Rough edges that show use. Not unfinished — worn. There's a difference.
What it should NOT feel like
A funnel
A redirect
A loading screen dressed up as content
An interstitial asking you to sign up before you continue
A billboard for Zone 3 — the path leads there, it doesn't advertise it
Copy direction
Active voice, present tense — you are moving through
Short. The path isn't where you read. It's where you walk.
Terrain language: trail, worn, groove, through, across, toward
Directional but not commanding — "the clearing is this way" not "click here to proceed"
Can name the transition explicitly: you are leaving the Lodge, you are entering the Clearing — the crossing is worth marking
The relationship to the Shallows
The Stomping Path delivers people to the Shallows. It does not compete with it. It does not try to be it. It earns the stillness of the Shallows by being its opposite — active, directional, in motion. By the time someone arrives at the Shallows via the Stomping Path, they have walked something. The arrival means more.

The thesis
The desire path that the watershed actually uses. Documentation of where people go when no one tells them where to go. Built for people who come back.
