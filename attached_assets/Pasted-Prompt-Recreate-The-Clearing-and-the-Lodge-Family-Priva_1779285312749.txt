What this document is
A plain-language privacy guide for families in a small homeschool community (~60 families). The community uses two tools: Sandbox (a private weekly community bulletin board — called "The Clearing") and Saltbox (a local-only homeschool day companion on each family's own device — called "The Lodge"). This guide explains how both tools work, where data lives, and gives families practical privacy habits in plain homestead language — no tech jargon.

There is one editable field: the community name, typed in the top-right corner. It persists in localStorage between visits (sandbox-handbook-community-name).

Section 1 — "The Clearing" (terracotta label)

The Clearing is the bounded gathering place your community keeps together — the Sandbox board. It holds this week's park days, field trips, asks, meetups, and the small news of the neighbourhood. Invitations are single-use. Posts reset each Sunday at midnight. The organizer can see everything on the board. No one outside your community can.

Section 2 — "The Lodge" (moss label)

The Lodge is your private home — Saltbox, on your own device. Portfolios, day notes, curricula, and family records live entirely on your device. Nothing leaves unless you open the door yourself. The Eave is the line between the Lodge and the Clearing: what stays below the Eave stays private.

Section 3 — "The key on the hook — sessions" (moss label)

Signing in hangs a key on the hook. It stays there for 30 days. If a device is lost or handed to someone outside the household, go to Settings and choose Sign out all devices — this changes the lock immediately. Every active session stops working. Then sign in fresh from your own device.

Section 4 — "The mailbox — Sunday digest" (moss label)

Your email address has your name on it and sits at the edge of the property — visible from the road. Add it in Settings to receive a warm summary each Sunday when the fresh board opens. Remove it any time. Removed means gone: it won't appear in the next digest. Your email is stored encrypted on the server.

Section 5 — "Three simple habits" (moss label)

Lock your screen when you set your device down. Sign out before passing your phone to someone outside your household. If you notice unfamiliar posts or activity, tell your organizer the same day.

Section 6 — "Zone identity and address hygiene" (terracotta label)

When you set up a zone identity in your settings, the tooling handles address rotation automatically — a fresh derived address is used for every send and every receive, so the same address never appears twice on-chain. Resting and standby are two states of the same master key root: you cannot have two separate zone identities, one for resting and one for participating. Your master key never leaves the device — it is encrypted with your passphrase and stored only locally.

Section 7 — "The covered wagon route — VPNs" (moss label)

A VPN changes the road, not the destination. Your internet provider (Rogers, Bell, Cogeco) sees you connect to the VPN — not where you went after. Sites you visit see the VPN's address, not yours. The VPN provider itself sees everything. They are your new road.

What a VPN does not do: protect a device that is already compromised at the OS level — spyware reads your data before it is encrypted, before it leaves. Prevent tracking once you log into an account. Make free services private — free VPNs have a business model, and it is usually your data.

Canada is a Five Eyes country (intelligence sharing with the US, UK, Australia, New Zealand). VPN providers in Five Eyes jurisdictions can be served legal orders requiring them to log and hand over your traffic. Many have. Providers in Switzerland or with independently verified no-logs audits are a meaningfully safer choice.

Section 8 — "The Canadian legal landscape" (moss label, no bottom margin — last section)

VPNs are currently legal in Canada. Bill C-26 (An Act respecting cybersecurity), introduced 2022 and still in Senate review as of 2025, gives the federal government broad authority to compel telecommunications providers to take "security-related" actions — including blocking specific services. No VPN ban has been enacted. The power exists in draft form.

What this means: using a VPN reduces what your ISP can see. It does not reduce what your device can see, what the VPN provider can see, or what websites collect once you're logged in. It is a useful layer for specific threats — not a complete answer. This summary is for awareness, not legal advice.
